## Why Credentials Matter for LASIK

LASIK is elective surgery. Unlike cataract surgery — where significant vision loss makes the decision straightforward — you are choosing to have a laser reshape your cornea when your vision is already functional with glasses or contacts. That makes surgeon selection a decision you should take seriously.

The stakes are real. A well-screened, well-executed LASIK procedure delivers 20/20 vision or better in over 95% of cases. A poorly screened one — where a cornea that was too thin or too steep slipped through — can cause regression, irregular astigmatism, or in rare cases, keratoconus progression. The surgery itself takes under ten minutes per eye. The screening and surgical judgement around it takes years to develop.

Credentials are a proxy for that judgement. Not a perfect one — but the best proxy you have before you've built trust with a surgeon.

## The Qualification Ladder in India

Understanding Indian ophthalmology qualifications helps you read a surgeon's profile accurately.

### MS Ophthalmology

The Master of Surgery (Ophthalmology) degree from a Medical Council of India-recognised university — Bombay University, AIIMS, JIPMER, or equivalent — is the gold-standard postgraduate qualification for ophthalmologists in India. It involves three years of supervised clinical and surgical training after MBBS. A surgeon with MS Ophthalmology has been formally trained in the full breadth of eye care: retina, cornea, glaucoma, oculoplastics, and paediatric ophthalmology.

For LASIK specifically, MS provides the foundational knowledge. It is not, by itself, proof that a surgeon is expert in refractive surgery — that comes from subspecialty training on top of MS.

### DNB Ophthalmology

The Diplomate of National Board (DNB) is the National Board of Examinations equivalent of a postgraduate degree. It is offered across accredited hospitals across India and is recognised by the Medical Council. DNB and MS are broadly equivalent in clinical standing. Many excellent refractive surgeons in Mumbai hold DNB rather than MS.

### Fellowship in Refractive or Corneal Surgery

This is where the real specialisation happens. After completing MS or DNB, surgeons who want to focus on refractive work pursue a fellowship — typically one to two years — under a mentor at a dedicated centre. These fellowships involve high surgical volumes, corneal subspecialty training, and hands-on work with laser platforms.

The fellowship is arguably more predictive of LASIK expertise than the MS/DNB degree itself.

### International Credentials: FRCS and FACS

- **FRCS (Fellow of the Royal Colleges of Surgeons, UK):** Awarded after passing a rigorous examination conducted by the Royal College of Ophthalmologists (or equivalent UK college). Internationally recognised and generally indicates the surgeon has been tested on a global standard of clinical knowledge and surgical competence.
- **FACS (Fellow of the American College of Surgeons):** Less common for ophthalmologists specifically; more relevant in other surgical disciplines. When seen in an ophthalmologist's profile, it usually indicates US training or fellowship.

International credentials add credibility and often indicate exposure to high-volume, technology-forward environments. They are a positive signal, not a requirement.

## Case Volume: What the Numbers Really Mean

Here is a simplified breakdown of what surgical volume means in practice:

| Case Volume | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Below 500 refractive cases | Early career; may be technically competent but limited pattern recognition |
| 500–2,000 cases | Adequate experience for standard presentations |
| 2,000–10,000 cases | Experienced; has managed a range of complex cases |
| 10,000+ refractive cases | Expert level; has seen and managed rare complications, edge cases, and complex prescriptions |

Volume matters because refractive surgery is highly pattern-dependent. A surgeon who has performed 10,000+ LASIK and SMILE procedures has encountered the full spectrum: the borderline cornea that needed to be turned away, the post-operative dry eye that needed escalating, the enhancement that was required six months later. That library of experience cannot be taught — it is accumulated case by case.

When evaluating a surgeon in Mumbai, ask directly: "How many refractive procedures have you personally performed?" Be aware that "our clinic has performed X procedures" is not the same as the individual surgeon's case count.

## Which Fellowship Programmes Produce the Best Refractive Surgeons in India

Several institutions have produced a disproportionate share of India's top corneal and refractive surgeons:

- **LV Prasad Eye Institute, Hyderabad:** One of India's most respected cornea and refractive fellowships. Alumni are found at leading centres across the country, including Mumbai.
- **Sankara Nethralaya, Chennai:** A high-volume centre with a rigorous cornea fellowship. Strong research output alongside clinical training.
- **Aravind Eye Hospital, Madurai/Coimbatore:** World-famous for volume and efficiency. Fellows trained here develop exceptional surgical flow and complication management.
- **AIIMS, New Delhi:** Academic fellowship with strong research grounding; particularly strong in complex cases.
- **International Fellowships (UK, Germany, USA):** Surgeons who have trained at centres like Moorfields Eye Hospital, London, or the Bascom Palmer Eye Institute, USA, bring exposure to technology and case volume that is hard to replicate domestically.

When a Mumbai-based LASIK surgeon mentions fellowship training at any of these institutions, it is a genuine credential — not marketing language.

## What Credentials Don't Tell You

A surgeon with an impressive CV can still deliver a mediocre consultation experience. Here is what letters after a name will not tell you:

**Marketing vs. real expertise.** Some clinics award their surgeons internal titles like "Senior Consultant" or "Head of Refractive Surgery" that have no external validation. These are organisational roles, not qualifications.

**Whether the surgeon personally performs your workup.** In many high-volume Mumbai clinics, the screening — the corneal topography, pachymetry, wavefront analysis — is performed by a technician or junior optometrist, and the surgeon reviews it briefly before surgery. The best surgeons do the workup themselves, or at a minimum, sit with you and walk through the maps personally.

**Whether they will tell you "no."** A surgeon with genuine expertise is comfortable turning patients away when the corneal profile is borderline. A surgeon under commercial pressure may push through cases that should not have been done. You cannot tell this from a degree certificate — you can tell it from how the consultation feels.

**Bedside manner.** This matters more than most patients acknowledge. A surgeon who cannot explain your corneal topography map in plain language, or who is dismissive of your questions, is not your best choice — regardless of their fellowship pedigree.

## How to Evaluate a Surgeon in Your Consultation

Your consultation is your audition of the surgeon. Here is what to observe in the twenty minutes that matter most:

### Does the surgeon look at your scans personally?

The Pentacam or Sirius corneal topography map is the single most important diagnostic tool in pre-LASIK screening. If your surgeon pulls it up, points to specific features, and explains what they mean for your surgery — that is the sign of an engaged clinician. If they glance at a summary printout and say "you're fine for LASIK," probe further.

### Can they explain your prescription in the context of your cornea?

A great refractive surgeon thinks about your entire optical system — not just your spectacle prescription. They should be able to tell you your corneal thickness, your K readings (the curvature of your cornea), and why those numbers make you a candidate or not.

### Do they offer you a genuine choice between procedures?

Standard LASIK, Femto LASIK, Contoura, SMILE, SILK — the right procedure depends on your corneal profile, your prescription, your lifestyle, and your risk tolerance. A surgeon who recommends the same procedure to every patient is not tailoring advice to you. A surgeon who walks through the options and explains the trade-offs is.

### Do they set realistic expectations?

95%+ of patients achieve 20/20 or better. But some patients end up at 20/25 or 20/30, and some need enhancements. A good surgeon tells you this upfront, including their own enhancement rate.

### Do they explain what happens if something goes wrong?

Ask directly: "What is your protocol if I develop significant dry eye or if my vision regresses?" How they answer this tells you a great deal about their patient care standards.

## The Difference Between a Good Surgeon and a Great One

A good LASIK surgeon in Mumbai will screen you appropriately, perform technically clean surgery, and send you home with drops. Most good surgeons deliver excellent results for straightforward cases.

A great LASIK surgeon does all of that — and also:

- Identifies the borderline case and either modifies the plan (choosing SMILE over LASIK for a thinner cornea, for example) or turns the patient away with a full explanation
- Personally interprets the wavefront and topography data rather than relying entirely on the automated report
- Has a published outcomes record — not marketing statistics, but peer-reviewed or audited data
- Maintains a genuine post-operative relationship with patients, not just a billing cycle
- Has invested in the latest laser platform and keeps it maintained, because they understand that the surgeon-machine combination produces the result

In Mumbai, the density of qualified ophthalmologists is high. The density of surgeons who meet all of the above criteria is considerably smaller. Your job as a patient is to find one of them.

## FAQ

### How do I find the best LASIK surgeon in Mumbai?

Start with credentials: look for MS Ophthalmology or DNB plus a fellowship in refractive or corneal surgery from a recognised institution (LV Prasad, Sankara Nethralaya, Aravind, or international equivalent). Then ask about personal case volume — you want a surgeon who has performed at least 2,000 refractive procedures personally, ideally 10,000+. Finally, evaluate the consultation: a great surgeon explains your corneal maps, offers you a genuine choice of procedures, and is comfortable telling you "no" if your cornea isn't suitable.

### What degree should a LASIK surgeon have?

In India, the baseline qualification is MS Ophthalmology (from a recognised university) or DNB Ophthalmology (National Board). For refractive surgery specifically, a fellowship in corneal or refractive surgery on top of that degree is the key differentiator. International credentials like FRCS (UK) add further credibility. No single degree guarantees a great outcome — but missing these baseline qualifications is a warning sign.

### How many LASIK surgeries is enough experience?

500–2,000 personal cases is considered adequate for straightforward presentations. 10,000+ cases is the threshold commonly used to define expert-level refractive surgeons. At that volume, a surgeon has encountered and managed the full range of outcomes including complications, enhancements, and complex prescriptions. Always ask for the surgeon's personal case count, not the clinic's total.

### Is FRCS important for a LASIK surgeon in Mumbai?

FRCS (Fellow of the Royal Colleges of Surgeons, UK) indicates the surgeon has passed a rigorous internationally benchmarked examination. It is a positive credential — it suggests formal training at an international standard and exposure to a broad case mix. It is not strictly required; many outstanding Mumbai LASIK surgeons have not pursued UK fellowship. But when present alongside a strong Indian fellowship and significant case volume, FRCS is a genuinely meaningful credential.

### Should the surgeon personally do the pre-LASIK workup?

In an ideal scenario, yes — or at minimum, the surgeon should personally review and explain your corneal topography maps with you before recommending a procedure. At high-volume clinics, technicians often perform the initial testing. What matters is whether the surgeon engages with those results meaningfully in your presence, not whether they pressed the button on the Pentacam.
